West Virginia has the highest rate of Medicaid enrollment of any state in the nation — over 30% of residents are covered by Medicaid — yet the state also has among the highest rates of medical debt, driven by its aging population, high chronic disease burden, and widespread poverty. ## Key West Virginia Consumer Protections

### Federal No Surprises Act (2022)

West Virginia does not have a separate state surprise billing law. All West Virginia patients with private insurance are protected by the federal No Surprises Act. Out-of-network providers at in-network facilities cannot bill more than your in-network cost-sharing amount for emergency care or non-emergency services without prior written consent.

### West Virginia Medicaid

West Virginia expanded Medicaid under the ACA, and coverage is extensive. West Virginia Medicaid is administered through managed care organizations (MCOs). Medicaid members cannot be balance billed for covered services. If you have WV Medicaid and received a bill above your required minimal cost-sharing, contact the West Virginia Department of Human Services.

### West Virginia Hospital Financial Assistance Requirements

Federal IRS law requires nonprofit West Virginia hospitals to maintain charity care programs. West Virginia's hospital landscape includes several large systems (WVU Medicine, Cabell Huntington Hospital, CAMC) and many small rural critical access hospitals — all nonprofit hospitals in both categories must have charity care programs.

### West Virginia Consumer Credit and Protection Act

The West Virginia Consumer Credit and Protection Act (W. Va. Code § 46A-1-101 et seq.) is one of the broader state consumer protection statutes and has been used to challenge deceptive debt collection practices. The AG enforces it and can seek civil penalties and restitution for WV consumers.

### West Virginia's Opioid Recovery Context

West Virginia has the highest opioid overdose death rate in the nation. Many West Virginians receive care related to substance use disorder treatment and recovery. Medical billing errors related to addiction treatment are common — including billing for treatment days not provided or upcoding treatment services. If you received substance use disorder treatment and received an unexpected bill, the standard dispute process applies.

## Who Regulates Medical Billing in West Virginia

### West Virginia Offices of the Insurance Commissioner (OIC)

The OIC regulates health insurance companies in West Virginia and handles consumer complaints about billing disputes, coverage denials, and claims practices.

- **Website:** wvinsurance.gov
- **Consumer Services:** 1-888-879-9842
- **File a Complaint:** wvinsurance.gov/consumer-complaint

For deceptive billing practices and violations of the WV Consumer Credit and Protection Act.

For West Virginia Medicaid billing complaints and appeals.

## How to Dispute a Medical Bill in West Virginia

**Step 1: Request your itemized bill.** Ask for a complete, line-by-line billing statement. Do not pay any bill before reviewing it in detail. Use our [EOB decoder](/eob-decoder) to compare your insurance Explanation of Benefits.

**Step 2: Identify errors.** Check for balance billing violations, duplicate charges, upcoded services, and charges for care not received. Our [billing errors guide](/billing-errors) covers the most common mistakes.

**Step 3: Apply for charity care.** Contact the hospital's billing department and ask specifically for a "financial assistance application" or "charity care form." West Virginia nonprofit hospitals must have these programs. Income thresholds often extend to 200–300% of the federal poverty level.

**Step 4: Submit a written dispute.** Use certified mail. Cite the federal No Surprises Act for balance billing. Use our [dispute letter tool](/dispute-letter) for a ready-made template.

**Step 5: File a grievance with your insurer.** West Virginia-regulated plans must have internal grievance procedures. File in writing and keep documentation.

**Step 6: Escalate to the WV OIC or AG.** File a complaint with the OIC for insurance-related issues or the AG's Consumer Protection Division for deceptive billing practices.

## West Virginia's Healthcare Challenges

West Virginia faces a perfect storm of factors that drive medical debt despite high Medicaid enrollment:

**Chronic disease burden:** West Virginia has the highest rates of heart disease, diabetes, and COPD of any state. Residents require frequent, expensive care, and cost-sharing on commercial insurance plans adds up quickly.

**Aging population:** West Virginia's population is aging rapidly as younger residents leave. Older patients on fixed incomes face significant exposure even with Medicare coverage — particularly for hospital co-insurance.

**Rural isolation:** West Virginia has 55 counties, many of them rural with limited provider options. Rural patients often have no practical choice but the nearest (sometimes out-of-network) provider. Federal No Surprises Act emergency protections are critical for these patients.

**Coal industry legacy:** Many West Virginians have employer-sponsored coverage through mining companies or their successors. These are often self-funded ERISA plans, meaning state insurance regulations don't apply — but federal protections do.

If you have a large West Virginia medical bill and limited income, the WV Legal Aid Society (wvlaids.org) provides free civil legal services, including medical debt assistance. Mountain State Justice (mountainstatejustice.org) also handles consumer debt cases.

## FAQ

**Q: I have West Virginia Medicaid. Why did I receive a bill?**
A: WV Medicaid members should not be billed for covered services beyond minimal required co-payments ($1–$4). If you received a bill, verify with your MCO that the service was covered. If it should be covered, contact your MCO's member services and then escalate to the WV Department of Human Services.

**Q: Does West Virginia have protections for patients who can't pay medical bills?**
A: The WV Consumer Credit and Protection Act protects consumers from deceptive and abusive debt collection. Collection agencies must comply with the federal FDCPA as well. If a collector uses harassment, false statements, or calls outside permitted hours, file a complaint with the WV AG and the CFPB.

**Q: Can a West Virginia hospital garnish my wages for medical debt?**
A: Yes, after a court judgment. West Virginia allows wage garnishment with some exemptions. Contact WV Legal Aid (wvlaids.org) or Mountain State Justice (mountainstatejustice.org) immediately if you receive a court summons about medical debt.

**Q: I received substance use disorder treatment and was billed for days I wasn't there. What do I do?**
A: Treatment billing errors are unfortunately common. Request your itemized bill, then request your treatment records to verify dates of service. If the bill includes days not supported by your records, submit a written dispute citing the discrepancy. File a complaint with the WV OIC and the WV AG if the provider doesn't correct it.

**Q: What's West Virginia's statute of limitations on medical debt?**
A: West Virginia's statute of limitations on written contracts is ten years (W. Va. Code § 55-2-6). This is longer than most states, so old medical bills can remain collectible for longer. This makes it especially important to dispute errors promptly rather than ignoring them.
