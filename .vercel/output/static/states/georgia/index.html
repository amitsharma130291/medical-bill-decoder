Key Georgia Consumer Protections

Federal No Surprises Act (2022)

The No Surprises Act applies to most Georgia patients with private insurance. It prohibits balance billing for emergency services and by out-of-network providers at in-network facilities. Georgia patients covered by these plans should not be charged more than their in-network cost-sharing for these situations.

Georgia’s Insurance Grievance Laws

Georgia insurance regulations (OC.G.A. Title 33) require health insurers to have grievance and appeal processes. Patients can file internal grievances with their plan and, if unresolved, file complaints with the Georgia Office of Commissioner of Insurance.

Hospital Charity Care

Georgia nonprofit hospitals are required by IRS rules to provide charity care. Many hospitals in Georgia — particularly large systems like Grady Health System in Atlanta, Emory Healthcare, and WellStar Health System — offer financial assistance programs based on income and household size.

Medical Debt and Collections

Georgia follows federal FDCPA rules for medical debt collection. Consumers have rights to request debt validation and dispute inaccurate debts in writing.

Who Regulates Medical Billing in Georgia

OCI regulates health insurance companies operating in Georgia and handles complaints about billing disputes, claim processing, and coverage denials.

How to Dispute a Medical Bill in Georgia

Step 1: Request your itemized bill and EOB. Contact your provider for a line-by-line itemized bill. Use our EOB decoder to decode your Explanation of Benefits from your insurer.

Step 2: Compare and identify discrepancies. Look for duplicate charges, services not received, upcoding, and balance billing issues. Our billing errors guide walks through each type.

Step 3: Contact the provider’s billing department. Send a written dispute identifying each problematic charge. Keep all records and confirmation of submissions.

Step 4: File a grievance with your insurer. Georgia-regulated health plans must have a grievance process. File in writing and request a written response.

Step 5: Escalate to OCI. If the dispute is not resolved within your plan’s timeframe, file a complaint with OCI. The Commissioner’s office can investigate and require corrective action.

Step 6: Seek external review. Georgia has an external review process for certain claim denials. Ask your insurer or OCI about eligibility for external review.

Georgia’s Nonprofit Hospital Charity Care Obligations

Georgia nonprofit hospitals receive substantial federal and state tax benefits in exchange for providing community benefit, including charity care. Under IRS requirements that apply in Georgia, nonprofit hospitals must:

  • Maintain and widely publicize a Financial Assistance Policy (FAP)
  • Not charge more than amounts generally billed (AGB) to insured patients for those who qualify for financial assistance
  • Not engage in “extraordinary collection actions” (lawsuits, liens, wage garnishment) without first making reasonable efforts to determine FAP eligibility

Grady Memorial Hospital’s charity care program is particularly notable: patients with incomes below 200% of the federal poverty level may qualify for free care, and those up to 400% FPL may receive sliding-scale discounts. The application requires income documentation but does not require citizenship verification.

Georgia also has a separate Medicaid program (Georgia Pathways) that provides coverage to certain adults who complete qualifying community engagement requirements — a unique structure that affects eligibility for billing assistance programs.

FAQ

Q: Are Georgia hospitals required to tell me about financial assistance before billing me? A: Nonprofit hospitals are required by IRS rules to have financial assistance policies and to make them widely available. However, active outreach varies. Always ask about financial assistance applications early in the billing process.

Q: Does Georgia follow the federal No Surprises Act for all private insurance plans? A: The federal law applies to most fully insured and self-insured employer-sponsored plans. Georgia-specific insurance regulations apply to state-regulated plans. ERISA self-insured plans follow federal rules.

Q: What is the timeline for Georgia insurer grievance responses? A: Georgia regulations require insurers to acknowledge grievances promptly and resolve standard grievances within 30 days, with expedited processing available for urgent situations.

Q: Can I get free help disputing a medical bill in Georgia? A: Yes. Georgia Legal Services Program and Atlanta Legal Aid Society offer free assistance to qualifying Georgia residents. Patient advocates at many Georgia hospitals also provide assistance at no charge.

Q: What happens if a Georgia provider sends my bill to collections? A: Under federal FDCPA protections, you can request debt validation within 30 days of the collector’s first contact. During this validation period, collection activity must pause. Send your dispute in writing via certified mail.
